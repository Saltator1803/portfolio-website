01 / TL;DR

The AI assistant market fractured — and that's the story.

ChatGPT's share of the AI assistant market fell below 50% for the first time in March 2026, down from a near-monopoly in 2023. Gemini's share nearly tripled — from 5.7% to 27.7% — not by being a better chatbot, but by being pre-installed on Android, embedded in Search's AI Overviews (2B+ monthly users), and bundled into Workspace. Meanwhile Microsoft Copilot barely registers in standalone web traffic (~1-3%) yet Techradar reports Copilot is far more popular than Gemini among CIOs for actual enterprise deployment. Three different distribution strategies, three different scoreboards.

02 / The Problem

When the underlying model stops being the differentiator, what do you compete on?

By 2026, frontier model benchmarks had converged — GPQA Diamond scores across leading assistants sit within a few points of each other. When capability becomes commoditized, the strategic question shifts from "whose model is smartest" to "who owns the distribution surface the user is already standing on." Google, Microsoft, and OpenAI each answered that question completely differently, and each answer implies a different long-term business.

03 / Context — only what matters

Three different games, three different scoreboards

  • ChatGPT: 900M weekly active users, 93% of Fortune 500 use OpenAI products — dominant on brand and first-mover consumer reach, but losing web-traffic share every quarter.
  • Gemini: 650M–900M monthly users via the app, plus 2B+ through AI Overviews in Search — winning by pre-installation and ecosystem bundling (Android, Workspace, Chrome), not by being sought out directly.
  • Copilot: ~420M monthly users across surfaces, only ~1-3% of standalone chatbot web traffic — but 74% user satisfaction in enterprise search and native Windows/Office 365 integration make it the default in Microsoft-shops regardless of consumer mindshare.
  • 81% of enterprises now run three or more model families concurrently — the "pick one winner" framing that dominated 2023–24 coverage no longer matches how companies actually buy AI.

04 / The Lens

Distribution is doing the work model quality used to do

01

Google: pre-installed, not sought out

Gemini's growth is inseparable from Android and Search bundling. The threat of substitution is low because switching away from Gemini means opting out of products users didn't choose to install it in.

02

Microsoft: invisible in consumer metrics, entrenched in enterprise

Copilot's governance maturity (Purview DLP, sensitivity labels, e-discovery) makes it the safe default for regulated industries — its moat is procurement risk-aversion, not user love.

03

OpenAI: brand equity depreciating in real time

ChatGPT's 19-point share drop in twelve months shows that first-mover brand recognition alone doesn't hold once credible, well-distributed alternatives exist — the closest thing to a cautionary tale in this case.

05 / The AI Angle

The real battle has moved to "who governs the agent," not "who has the model"

As enterprises adopt three-plus model families simultaneously, the strategic prize shifts from model supremacy to governance and orchestration — who can safely let an AI agent take action across a company's tools without it becoming a compliance nightmare. This is why Microsoft's Copilot, despite trailing badly on consumer web traffic, remains the safest bet for large regulated enterprises: it isn't competing on "best chatbot," it's competing on "least likely to get IT fired."

06 / My Call

Stop competing for "primary assistant." Compete for a specific workflow.

If I were product-leading any of these three, I would stop measuring success by share-of-chatbot-market — that metric rewards vanity and punishes the companies (like Microsoft) actually winning the higher-value enterprise deals. I'd instead pick one or two vertical workflows (legal document review, financial reconciliation, customer support triage) and make the AI assistant the undisputed best tool for that specific job, rather than a mediocre generalist competing on brand recall.

The tradeoff I'd accept: lower headline user numbers and less press coverage, in exchange for defensible, high-margin revenue in workflows where "good enough" competitors can't dislodge you.

Developer Perspective: Having worked inside a codebase with legacy integrations, I'd note that Copilot's advantage isn't the model — it's that embedding it across Word, Excel, and Teams means integrating with years of existing enterprise backend (Graph API, permissions, legacy auth) in a way a standalone consumer app like Gemini never has to.

07 / How I'd Measure It

North Star + two supporting signals

★ North Star
Task-completion rate for the chosen workflow, not general chatbot session count
Signal 01
Enterprise seat retention after 12 months — proxy for whether the tool became infrastructure or stayed a novelty
Signal 02
% of customers running the assistant alongside a competitor — honest measure of whether you're the default or just one of several

In AI, the platform that wins isn't the one with the best model — it's the one already installed on your phone before you had an opinion.
